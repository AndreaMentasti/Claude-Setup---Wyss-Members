Finding 1 — Villain treatment works on AI. The villain prime increases the villain rate from 60% (neutral) to 83% (+23pp) and villain entity share from 24% to 37.5%. This is the clearest treatment effect in the pilot.
Finding 2 — Hero→Victim boomerang effect. The hero prime does not increase hero framing (50% = same as neutral), but dramatically increases victim framing (60% vs 20% in neutral, +40pp). Priming "AI as hero" appears to activate awareness of who AI might harm — a narrative boomerang. Hero entity share drops from 20% to 16%, while victim share jumps from 12% to 26%.

2.3 Narrative DAG — AI

2.4 Thematic Clusters in AI Responses

Reading the responses, five recurring themes emerge:

  1. Job displacement / economic threat — The most common villain narrative. Workers, "the workforce", "common man" as victims. AI as the agent of displacement. Present in ~40% of responses.
  2. Misinformation / epistemic threat — AI as producer of fake content, images, voices. Society / "ordinary people" as victims. "It's not required to be labeled as AI so it's believed to be real." Present in ~25% of responses.
  3. Energy / environmental cost — AI data centers, electricity costs, resource drain. Often coded as neutral or villain depending on emphasis. Present in ~20% of responses.
  4. AI as personal tool / hero — Positive experiences with Gemini, ChatGPT, practical problem-solving. "I don't know how I lived without AI." Pure hero framing, no ambivalence. Present in ~25% of responses.
  5. Unregulated corporate power — Tech companies, "companies who are building these models", government/corporations as villains. A systemic rather than technological framing. Present in ~15% of responses.

Finding 3 — Villain treatment suppresses villain framing on immigration (reactance). The villain prime produces the lowest villain rate (53%) and the highest neutral rate (52.6% of entities). The neutral arm produces the highest villain rate (75%). This is a classic reactance pattern: liberal-leaning respondents pushed back against the villain frame by writing more neutrally or redirecting the villain label toward the government rather than immigrants.
Finding 4 — Hero prime activates full narrative engagement. The hero treatment is the only condition producing zero neutral entities (0%) and simultaneously high villain (37.5%), hero (25%), and victim (37.5%) shares. It "turns on" the narrative structure completely — respondents write richer, more role-saturated text.

3.2 The Dual-Frame Problem

Core structural issue: "Immigrants" appears as villain, hero, AND victim across respondents — the same entity maps to three different roles depending on political frame. The villain binary (0/1) collapses two fundamentally opposite narratives into a single code.

3.3 Thematic Clusters in Immigration Responses

  1. "Nation of immigrants" founding myth — Invoked in ~60% of responses, across all treatments. Functions as a shared moral frame that both sides use differently.
  2. Legal vs illegal distinction — The most common structural frame (~50% of responses). Even pro-immigration respondents draw this line. Suggests "legal immigration = OK, illegal = problem" is a cross-ideological baseline.
  3. Humanitarian/compassion frame — Present in ~40% of responses, strongly associated with liberal-frame respondents. Immigrants fleeing danger, deserving dignity.
  4. Economic burden frame — "They come for our jobs", "we can't take care of our own". Present in ~20% of conservative-frame responses.
  5. Current events / Trump/ICE saliency — ~30% of responses reference the current deportation situation unprompted. Immigration is uniquely contaminated by real-world events at time of survey (March 2026).

The co-occurrence matrix shows how often multiple roles appear in the same response. For AI, villain+victim co-occur in 11 responses — the "AI harms people" narrative. 5. Implications for Full Pilot Design

  1. AI is the better topic for measuring villain narrative activation. The causal chain is clean: villain prime → villain framing of AI (+23pp). Use AI as the primary DV topic in the full pilot.
  2. Add ideology/party ID as a moderator for immigration. The treatment effect on immigration is entirely masked by political identity. A 2×2 design (treatment × ideology) would be essential to identify within-frame effects.
  3. The hero→victim boomerang deserves a hypothesis. This is a novel finding: hero priming activates victim awareness. Possible mechanism: "If AI is a hero, who is it saving? → identifies victims." Test in full pilot with larger N.
  4. Villain share (entity-level) is a richer DV than villain=0/1. Use the individual-level villain share (villain entities / total entities per respondent) as a continuous DV. More sensitive and avoids ceiling effects.
  5. Separate villain direction on immigration. Add a follow-up question: "Who or what is the main problem with immigration in the US?" Or code villain entities by target (immigrants vs government) rather than just presence/absence.
  6. Headline task has low sensitivity. 66% neutral entity rate and reactance pattern. Consider replacing with a "rate this story" task, a forced-choice framing question, or a longer writing task that gives more signal.
  7. Increase immigration Neutral/Hero cell sizes. Currently n=4 each — essentially no power. Aim for n≥30 per cell in the full pilot.
